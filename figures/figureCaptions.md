**Figure 4. A shared-benign reference separates a conserved monocyte-immunosuppression core from a ccRCC-skewed complement–macrophage programme.** Myeloid module scores were computed per cell and aggregated to the patient level in clear-cell renal cell carcinoma (ccRCC) and prostate cancer (PCa), each contrasted against the same panel of benign bone-marrow donors. (a) Tumor induction of each module, expressed as the standardized mean difference (in units of SD) between tumor and shared-benign cells, in ccRCC (blue) and PCa (orange); both cancers induce the ATF3/NF-κB and complement/C1Q programmes. (b) Cancer-type × condition interaction coefficients from mixed-effects models, where positive values indicate stronger tumor induction in ccRCC; bars are coloured blue where the interaction is significant (p < 0.05) and grey otherwise. (c) Within PCa tumors, module scores in tumor-associated macrophages and monocytes (TAM/TIM, green) versus classical monocytes (grey); asterisks denote two-sided Wilcoxon signed-rank significance.

**Figure 5. The CLEC_LAM complement programme is macrophage-derived and receives predicted APOE–TREM2 and complement signalling.** (a) Mean log-normalized expression of the complement C1q chains C1QA, C1QB and C1QC across cell classes in the ccRCC bone-marrow niche; expression is largely restricted to the CLEC_LAM state and other tumor-associated macrophages (TAM). (b) CellChat-predicted signalling to the CLEC_LAM state, with senders on the horizontal axis and ligand–receptor pairs on the vertical axis; dot colour and dot size both encode the communication probability. (c) NicheNet ligand prioritization for the CLEC_LAM transcriptional response, ranked by corrected area under the precision–recall curve (AUPR); complement-family ligands are highlighted in teal and each ligand's rank among 116 candidates is annotated. (d) Uniform manifold approximation and projection (UMAP) of ccRCC myeloid cells coloured by the continuous complement_C1Q module score. TIM, tumor-infiltrating monocyte; MSC, mesenchymal stromal cell.

**Figure 6. The complement_C1Q programme marks an immune-dysfunctional, prognostically adverse ccRCC phenotype.** (a) Adjusted association (standardized coefficient β with 95% confidence interval) between the complement_C1Q score and CD8+ T-cell cytotoxicity in ccRCC (blue, n = 24 samples) and PCa (orange, n = 31); each model adjusts for TAM fraction, CD8 fraction, malignant fraction and condition. The association is significant in ccRCC only (β = −0.82, p = 0.039) and is null in PCa (β = +0.003, p = 0.99). (b) Adjusted associations (standardized coefficient with 95% confidence interval) between complement_C1Q and CD8+ cytotoxicity, CD8+ exhaustion and MHC-II antigen presentation in ccRCC. (c) Overall-survival (OS) hazard ratios per standard deviation (95% CI) for myeloid programmes in TCGA-KIRC (n = 532; 175 events), each adjusted for panTAM abundance, the Obradovic TREM2 signature and immune infiltration; hazard ratios whose 95% CI excludes 1 are shown in blue. (d,e) Kaplan–Meier overall survival by median-split myeloid-derived risk score in (d) TCGA-KIRC (n = 604, 202 deaths) and (e) the independent CPTAC ccRCC cohort (n = 110, 21 deaths). Shaded bands are 95% confidence intervals; p-values are from two-sided log-rank tests; the tables below each panel give the number of patients remaining at risk at the indicated time points.

**Figure 7. A myeloid-derived 25-gene signature stratifies ccRCC survival and transfers to prostate cancer.** (a) LASSO-Cox coefficients of the 25 signature genes; positive coefficients (blue) increase and negative coefficients (orange) decrease the risk score. (b) Discrimination, showing the Harrell concordance index (bars) and time-dependent area under the curve (AUC, points) in the TCGA-KIRC training and held-out sets and the independent CPTAC ccRCC cohort. AUCs are shown at 1, 3 and 5 years for the TCGA cohorts; in CPTAC, whose median follow-up is 3.0 years, only the 1- and 3-year AUCs were estimable. The fourth bar (purple) is the combined nomogram (signature plus stage, grade and age) in the TCGA-KIRC held-out set; the other bars are the signature alone. (c) Held-out concordance of the signature compared with the Obradovic TREM2 signature, the CLEC_LAM core and a panTAM score. Each comparator gene set was refit through the identical pipeline used for the signature (same training split, univariate screen and LASSO-Cox with 10-fold cross-validation), so the comparison reflects gene content rather than model fitting. The grey band spans 0.5 to the 95th percentile of a null distribution of 500 random gene sets of equal size drawn from the same candidate pool with randomly assigned weights. (d) Cross-tumor validation in TCGA-PRAD, showing the hazard ratio per standard deviation (95% CI) of the signature for progression-free interval (PFI) and biochemical recurrence (BCR).

**Figure S1. Construction and clinical calibration of the ccRCC risk model.** (a) Ten-fold cross-validated concordance (left axis, blue) and number of non-zero genes (right axis, grey) along the LASSO-Cox regularization path; the dashed line marks the selected penalty. (b) Calibration of predicted versus observed 3-year mortality across risk quartiles; the dashed line denotes perfect calibration. (c) Decision-curve analysis showing the net benefit of the risk model relative to treat-all and treat-none strategies at a 3-year horizon. (d) Contribution of each covariate to the multivariable Cox model, expressed as points per standard deviation; asterisks denote Wald-test significance.

**Figure S2. Therapeutic and immune landscape of the frozen ccRCC risk groups.** High- and low-risk groups were defined by the median of the frozen 25-gene score in TCGA-KIRC (n = 604; 302 per group). (a) Difference in oncoPredict-imputed log-IC50 (high − low) for five ccRCC agents (sunitinib, pazopanib, axitinib, cabozantinib and temsirolimus); negative values indicate greater predicted drug sensitivity in the high-risk group. (b) Difference in Tumor Immune Dysfunction and Exclusion (TIDE) scores and their components between risk groups. (c) Over-representation analysis (Enrichr) of the 144 TAM-versus-monocyte differentially expressed genes; bars show −log10 Benjamini–Hochberg-adjusted p and the dotted line marks adjusted p = 0.05. (d) Difference in immune-infiltration signature scores between risk groups. In (a), (b) and (d), group differences were tested by two-sided Wilcoxon rank-sum test; blue bars are significant after Benjamini–Hochberg correction (FDR < 0.05) and grey bars are not.

**Figure S3. Copy-number inference confirms that the complement-producing macrophages are non-malignant.** Single-cell copy-number variation (CNV) was inferred with inferCNV for 7,347 cells from ccRCC bone-metastasis tumour samples, using T and natural killer (T/NK) cells as the diploid reference; myeloid cells were deliberately excluded from the reference so that their ploidy remained a tested observation. (a) Per-cell CNV burden, defined as the mean squared deviation of denoised expression residuals from 1, by compartment; boxes show the median and interquartile range and the dashed line marks the median of the T/NK reference. (b) Median CNV burden per compartment expressed as fold change relative to the T/NK reference. (c) Median CNV burden of malignant versus myeloid cells within each patient sample; the diagonal denotes equality. Cells labelled malignant carried the highest burden of any compartment (3.83-fold the reference) and myeloid cells the lowest of all observed compartments (1.93-fold); the malignant-versus-myeloid difference was 1.99-fold (two-sided Mann–Whitney p = 7.5 × 10⁻²⁰⁷) and held in all seven samples with sufficient cells.
